Genesis

Let's start at the beginning.

ü™ê + üíª

As I mentioned, I love astronomy and I have been trying to do some coding and apps related to it for a while.

But when I started, only a few data were available online, often in a format that was not easy to use.

This is for example a screenshot from the great website Heavens Above, which provides a lot of data about the sky. The work done by the author Chris Peat is amazing, but as you can see this is HTML. If I wanted to access this data, I would have to scrape the website, which is not very nice, and quite a pain to do.

Sharing is caring

I like the idea that astronomical data are universal and should be available to everyone. So I started investigating how Chris Peat was getting was able to produce such precise data and if I could do it myself.

As the idea grew on me, I started thinking that it would be great to have the code open source so that someone else could do the same if they wanted. And to try to explain how celestial mechanics work while I learnt some of it.

This is how I started Astronoby

Astronoby is an open source Ruby library that provides astronomical data and events 


  mars = Astronoby::Mars.new(instant: instant, ephem: ephem)

  topocentric = mars.observed_by(geneva)

  horizontal = topocentric.horizontal

  puts "Mars is #{horizontal.altitude.round(2)}¬∞
        high in the sky"

It enables to compute the position of planets, times of when they rise and set, Moon phases and much more.

I'm not going to tell the whole story but basically Astronoby started out of many books with mathematical formulas on how to compute data and events.

And this is the first thing about side projects.

It takes forever

It takes forever and you only have so much time.

Either it is done on personal time or work time, both are limited.

But that's not the obstacle.

Who needs this?
Who needs to compute Moon phases in Ruby? Basically, nobody cares. I'm not saying this a bitter way, this just an objective fact. It's already possible to do the same in Python for years, with way better performance and features.
I'm not doing this right

Talking about doing it right, this is the classical imposter syndrome. I am not an astronomer or a scientist, how can I pretend to calculate this data right? Who is going to trust me?

  • This is hard and I'm not a real scientist (imposer syndrom)
    • Example: geocentric to topocentric coordinates is way more complex than anticipated
    • Example: turning barycentric vectors into topocentric coordinates, how am I supposed to know how to do that?
  • I could spend this time working on business ideas instead
  • Before ephemeris: results are just not good enough to compete, what's the point?
So much effort and no feedback

I wrote this blog post after the biggest refactoring of Astronoby which took me months and enabled the biggest precision improvement and many planets-related features.

I barely got any feedback, no likes, no comments. I said it earlier, nobody cares about this. There is a limited interest for this.

I could be more productive with my time

This one depends on people. I have friends who spend their personal time trying to start a side business and at some point it got me thinking that this is maybe not the most productive way to spend my personal time.

The unexisting value of niche open-source projects

Well, this is probably a turn of events you didn't expect.

I'm joking, of course. It is natural to feel this way, I believe we all do at some point, but my main point today is the next part, that there is actually a lot of value in these projects.

Staying motivated

First, let's see how we can boost and keep the motivation.

Passion

This one is easy and we tend to forget it. Why did you start working on this project in the first place? You actually like this project. It is exciting and you are having a good time.

We could actually stop here. I mean, if you are having a good time, why would you stop?

But things get more interesting when you start thinking about everything this project brings you.

Personal mission and challenge

When I started my studies, I didn't know what I wanted to do. It turns out programming was giving me the same thrill of solving problems as my high school math exercises. This might be just me, I know I'm weird. But the sense of challenge, and the will to achieve my goal of making providing astronomical data in Ruby, that got me excited.

Set up achievable goals

Celebrate wins

Talk about it

It can help to add a tiny bit of project management. Think of small thresholds that you can achieve, and celebrate them.

You can also use the community to help you stay motivated as a witness of your progress. When I wanted to lose weight, I told others I started eating healthier, through accountability I was more likely to stick to it. Well, it didn't work, but it was worth trying.

Look at what already worked

The community is full of examples of people who started niche projects and made them successful. For example, the Faker gem, started almost 20 years ago, initially a very niche need, now a very popular gem with hundreds of individual contributors, thousands of stars, used for testing, seeding, development, fun and more.

Unlocking personal and professional growth

And now comes the most interesting part, the hidden value of niche open-source projects is that it makes you grow.

Open-source best practices

First, you're going to learn a lot about open-source development.

What is versioning, how do you deal with breaking changes?

How do you manage dependencies and handle your own gem as a dependency for others?

How important is documentation? How to maintain a changelog?

How to write maintainable code? How to make it readable?

How can you make your library accessible to everyone?

Open-source is not just about writing public code. Well, this is an important part with a lot of value for the community already, but it is more than that. It is about sharing knowledge, collaborating, and building a community around your project.

An open-source library means people can use it, look into it, contribute to it, have opinions, and build on top of it.

By making your project an open-source library, you are contributing to the community and you become responsible for some that didn't exist before.

New technical skills

With your code being public, you might want to make it better, more efficient, more elegant. Either you are already a very experienced developer and you are going to show-off your skills. Or, you are going to learn on the way.

How can I apply object-oriented programming principles to my algorithm? How can I design a nice API for the end-user? What if I tryapply domain-driven design principles to my code? What's the best way to test my code?

Your project is an awesome opportunity to try and learn new things, what interests you and when you decide to work on it.

New relations, new clients, new job opportunities

It doesn't matter if it's niche, you actually have a lot to say and you're one of the few people who can say it.

While work on your project, there are opportunities to share your ideas and your progress. You are going to have many blog post ideas. Why not talk about them in a podcast? Maybe do a meetup presentations.

Or even a conference talk?

This is going to make you meet people, expand your visibility and create new opportunities.

Last year, someone contacted me on a social network about Astronoby, as they wanted to use it in their project. While I didn't get work with them specifically on Astronoby, I ended up as a consultant for thoughtbot on their project. This can be an idea to sell open source development time at your company, it might bring new client opportunities.

One more point, you don't have to take yourself seriously. If this is niche and you feel like you are all alone doing your thing, it also means that you can take your time, try things, make mistakes. Nobody is expecting anything from you, so you can focus on what you want, either it's the code, the features, the sharing, the community, the fun, or all of it.

Who needs this?

You are not alone

As we often say, if it's useless, then it's indensible. The fact that you are doing it means that there is at least one person who needs it. And you will be surprised to see how many people are actually sharing your interest.

Here screenshot of Hello Weather

I said earlier that nobody cares about computing Moon phases in Ruby. Turns out there is a weather application that very much needs it. And today, Astronoby is used in production by Hello Weather, a weather application with many users.
I'm not doing this right

I agree this one is hard, we often want to do things right and we are afraid of making mistakes. But there are many definitions of "right". By doing it your way, you are bringing your own style, your own vision. This might become the new "right" way.
