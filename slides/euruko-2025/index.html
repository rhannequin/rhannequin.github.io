Genesis

ü™ê + üíª

Let's start at the beginning.

I love astronomy and I have been trying to do some coding and apps related to it for a while.

But when I started, only a few data were available online, often in a format that was not easy to use.

This is for example a screenshot from the great website Heavens Above, which provides a lot of data about the sky. The work done by the author Chris Peat is amazing, but as you can see this is HTML. If I wanted to access this data, I would have to scrape the website, which is not very nice, and quite a pain to do.

Sharing is caring

I like the idea that astronomical data are universal and should be available to everyone. So I started investigating how Chris Peat was able to produce such precise data and if I could do it myself.

As the idea grew on me, I started thinking that it would be great to have the code open source so that someone else could do the same if they wanted. And to try to explain how celestial mechanics work while I learnt some of it.

This is how I started Astronoby

Astronoby is an open source Ruby library that provides astronomical data and events 


  mars = Astronoby::Mars.new(instant: instant, ephem: ephem)

  topocentric = mars.observed_by(geneva)

  horizontal = topocentric.horizontal

  puts "Mars is #{horizontal.altitude.round(2)}¬∞
        high in the sky"

It enables to compute the position of planets, times of when they rise and set, Moon phases and much more.

I'm not going to tell the whole story but basically Astronoby started out of many books with mathematical formulas on how to compute data and events.

At this point you might be thinking "This is a nice story R√©my, but how is it going to help me?"

This is where things got complicated.

It took me almost 2 years to release the first minor version of Astronoby.

And this is the first thing about side projects.

Who needs this?
Who needs to compute Moon phases in Ruby? Basically, nobody cares. I'm not saying this in a bitter way, it's just an objective fact. It's already possible to do the same in Python for years, with way better performance and features.
I'm not doing this right

Talking about doing it right, this is the classical imposter syndrome. I am not an astronomer or a scientist, how can I pretend to calculate this data right? Who is going to trust me?

So much effort and no feedback

I wrote this blog post after the biggest refactoring of Astronoby which took me months and enabled the biggest precision improvement and many planets-related features.

I barely got any feedback, no likes, no comments. I said it earlier, nobody cares about this. There is a limited interest for this.

The hidden no value of niche open-source projects

Well, this is probably a turn of events you didn't expect.

I'm joking, of course. It is natural to feel this way, I believe we all do at some point, but my main point today is the next part, that there is actually a lot of value in these projects.

Staying motivated

First, let's see how we can boost and keep the motivation.

Work with passion
when passion is your work

This one is easy and we tend to forget it. Why did you start working on this project in the first place? You actually like this project. It is exciting and you are having a good time.

We could actually stop here. I mean, if you are having a good time, why would you stop?

But things get more interesting when you start thinking about everything this project brings you.

Personal mission and challenge

When I started my studies, I didn't know what I wanted to do. It turns out programming was giving me the same thrill of solving problems as my high school math exercises. This might be just me, I know I'm weird. But the sense of challenge, and the will to achieve my goal of providing astronomical data in Ruby, that got me excited.

Look at what already worked

  • Faker
  • SQLite
  • Cowsay

The community is full of examples of people who started niche projects and made them successful.

For example, the Faker gem, started almost 20 years ago, initially a very niche need, now a very popular gem with hundreds of individual contributors, thousands of stars, used for testing, seeding, development, fun and more.

SQLite it started as a small library to provide a lightweight database engine, and now it is even used by NASA on a Mars rover.

Cowsay started as a joke, and now it is used by CI and CD as a status notifier and an easter egg in operating systems.

Unlocking personal and professional growth

And now comes the most interesting part, the hidden value of niche open-source projects is that it makes you grow.

Open-source best practices

Versioning, documentation, code quality

First, you're going to learn a lot about open-source development.

What is versioning, how do you deal with breaking changes?

How do you manage dependencies and handle your own gem as a dependency for others?

How important is documentation? How to maintain a changelog?

How to write maintainable code? How to make it readable?

Open-source is not just about writing public code. Well, this is an important part with a lot of value for the community already, but it is more than that. It is about sharing knowledge, collaborating, and building a community around your project.

An open-source library means people can use it, look into it, contribute to it, have opinions, and build on top of it.

By making your project an open-source library, you are contributing to the community and you become responsible for some that didn't exist before.

New technical skills

With your code being public, you might want to make it better, more efficient, more elegant. Either you are already a very experienced developer and you are going to show-off your skills. Or, you are going to learn on the way.

How can I apply object-oriented programming principles to my algorithm? How can I design a nice API for the end-user? What if I try to apply domain-driven design principles to my code? What's the best way to test my code? 


    angle1 = Astronoby::Angle.as_radians(Math::PI)
    angle2 = Astronoby::Angle.as_degrees(90)

    angle1.to_degrees.value == angle2.value

Let me give you a couple examples of how I used Astronoby to how I applied some of these principles.

This code comes from Astronoby v0.1.0, the first working version. To deal with angles, I had dedicated classes that were responsible for converting themselves from one unit to another.

In use, it was not very convenient, as you had to convert angles to the same unit before comparing them. 


    angle1 = Astronoby::Angle.from_radians(Math::PI)
    angle2 = Astronoby::Angle.from_degrees(90)

    angle1 == angle2

Then, I introduced value objects, a design pattern that encapsulates a value and its unit, so that you can compare angles regardless of their unit.

Note that I also changed the method names to reflect that an angle stays an angle, regardless of its unit. 


            time = Astronoby::Epoch.to_utc(sun.epoch)

Here's another example, this time to illustrate how I applied domain-driven design principles to Astronoby.

The first way to get the altitude in degrees of the Sun was long with many conversions, different ways to deal with time, inconsistent naming. 


                sun

Then, I focused on making a nice API for the end-user, oriented towards the domain. The developer doesn't have to deal with conversions, they just have to call the methods that make sense in the context of the problem they are trying to solve.

Your project is an awesome opportunity to try and learn new things, what interests you and when you decide to work on it.

New relations, new clients, new job opportunities

Another great thing about side projects is that they create opportunities.

It doesn't matter if it's niche, you actually have a lot to say and you're one of the few people who can say it.

While working on your project, there are opportunities to share your ideas and your progress. You are going to have many blog post ideas. Why not talk about them in a podcast? Maybe do a meetup presentations.

Or even a conference talk?

This is going to make you meet people, expand your visibility and create new opportunities.

Last year, someone contacted me on a social network about Astronoby, as they wanted to use it in their project. While I didn't get work with them specifically on Astronoby, I ended up as a consultant for thoughtbot on their project. This can be an idea to sell open source development time at your company, it might bring new client opportunities.

One more point, you don't have to take yourself seriously. If this is niche and you feel like you are all alone doing your thing, it also means that you can take your time, try things, make mistakes. Nobody is expecting anything from you, so you can focus on what you want, either it's the code, the features, the sharing, the community, the fun, or all of it.

And if you don't do side projects, that is very fine too. There many other occasions where perseverance and passion is welcome.

You are having fun

So, you are not wasting your time. You are having fun, you are learning, you are growing. You don't need appreciation to have a good time.

Who needs this?

You are not alone

You are the Ruby community

As we sometimes say, if it's useless, then it's indispensable. The fact that you are doing it means that there is at least one person who needs it. And you will be surprised to see how many people are actually sharing your interest.

Maybe we do need to be able to compute astronomical data in Ruby. Ruby is such a great language, so expressive, so elegant, but yet it is not used much in the scientific community. That can change now. We are all part of the Ruby community.

I said earlier that nobody cares about computing Moon phases in Ruby. Turns out there is a weather application that very much needs it. And today, Astronoby is used in production by Hello Weather, a weather application with many users.
I'm not doing this right

You are doing it your way, an original way

I agree this one is hard, we often want to do things right and we are afraid of making mistakes. But there are many definitions of "right". By doing it your way, you are bringing your own style, your own vision. This might become the new "right" way.

This one is my favourite because all your efforts are paying off in many ways you didn't expect. You are learning, you are growing, you are meeting people, you are having fun. This is very much worth it.

Conclusion

I hope I didn't turn this talk into some kind of life-style program. All I wanted is to share my experience.

There are many obstacles

If you love it, do it

There's so much to take on the way

It's true it's not always easy. You're going to face moments of doubt, you might want to give up and you might feel like it is not worth it.

But if you love it, if you are having fun, if you are learning, then do it. You will be surprised by how much you can learn and how much you can grow.

One day you might receive a GitHub issue with bad news and you will be incredibly happy just to have someone using your project.
